Trustworthy Embodied Foundation Models:
Safety by Design versus Evaluation

RSS 2026, Sydney, Australia | July 13-17


Overview

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models promise general-purpose robotic agents that can perceive their environment, understand instructions, and act in the physical world. These models leverage large-scale visual and textual training data and are fine-tuned to control robots across many tasks. The result is impressive breadth: single policies can fold laundry, set tables, fetch objects, and more. However, with this broad capability comes significant safety and robustness challenges. Recent studies are revealing that current VLA-based agents, despite their capabilities, can be brittle and sometimes unsafe when faced with the messy unpredictability of the real world [1, 2].

This workshop aims to bring together researchers and practitioners working on robotics foundation models (FMs) to initiate a discussion on safety and robustness for general-purpose embodied agents. We frame the space through two complementary lenses: safety by design (reducing risk at the source) and safety by evaluation (deployment-time evaluation and mitigation).

Robotics foundation models promise broad, general-purpose behavior, including out-of-the-box generalization and increasingly complex skills such as dexterous manipulation (e.g., laundry folding, barista-style tasks) and in-the-wild navigation and planning [1-3]. However, this breadth also brings new safety challenges. FM-based agents can misinterpret or hallucinate task-relevant details, fail to properly ground language in the physical scene, and struggle with real-world execution (e.g., contact dynamics, occlusions) [4-6]. They are also susceptible to adversarial jailbreak-style prompting that can induce unsafe behaviors [7]. In embodied settings, these failures can translate to unsafe motion, damaged property, and risky interactions with people, leading to loss of trust and reliability.

This workshop is timely because it will consolidate insights from recent works (2024â€“2026) on robust robotic policies. More importantly, one goal of this workshop is to start a community discussion around a shared taxonomy and toolbox for trustworthy embodied foundation models, organized around two complementary perspectives: (1) Safety by design, focusing on reducing risk at the source (e.g., training data, architectures, and objectives), and (2) Safety by practice, focusing on deployment-time evaluation and mitigation (e.g., runtime monitoring, red-teaming, shielding, and robust failure recovery systems).

The intended audience includes researchers and industry practitioners in robot learning, embodied AI, manipulation, and navigation, as well as the verification, controls, and HRI communities interested in accountable autonomy. We will draw presenters and panelists from sub-communities spanning robotics foundation models, robustness and evaluation, formal methods and runtime assurance, control and recovery, and human-in-the-loop autonomy deployed in real-world robotic domains.

We want the RSS community to actively engage with the safety implications of FM-based robots before such systems become mainstream in homes, hospitals, and workplaces. This workshop hopes to push towards consensus on evaluation standards and foster collaborations that address the key safety bottlenecks, helping shape the next generation of embodied AI on a safe, trustworthy foundation.

Areas of Interest

We solicit papers of the following topics:

Safety by design

  • Training data, architectures, and objectives that reduce risk at the source
  • Robust training regimens using real or simulated data (e.g., adversarial training, domain randomization)
  • Formal methods and guarantees
Safety by evaluation

  • Benchmarking for embodied safety evaluation
  • Runtime assurance: monitoring, uncertainty/OOD detection, shielding, and safe fallback policies
  • Lessons from red-teaming and jailbreaking of VLA models
  • Continual learning for enhanced failure detection and recovery
  • Human-in-the-loop systems for intervention, recovery, and incorporating failure feedback
  • Standardization for safety deployments in real-world/industrial applications
  • Explainability for building trust in human-robot collaborations
